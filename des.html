The Digital Image

A technical guide to formation, formats, and the history of visual data.

1. Image Formation & Creation

At its core, a digital image is a numerical representation of a visual scene. In Computer Science, we treat an image as a Discrete 2D Function f(x, y), where x and y are spatial coordinates.

The Process:

  • Acquisition: Light passes through a lens and hits a CMOS Sensor.
  • Sampling: The continuous light is broken into a grid of discrete points called Pixels (Picture Elements).
  • Quantization: The intensity of light in each pixel is converted into a digital value (typically 0 to 255 for 8-bit images).

For color images, we use the RGB Color Space, where each pixel consists of three stacked layers (channels): Red, Green, and Blue.

2. Digital Image Types

Raster Images

Made of a fixed grid of pixels. When scaled up, they lose quality (pixelation). Examples: Photos, AI generations.

Vector Images

Based on mathematical paths (points, lines, curves). They are infinitely scalable without quality loss. Used for logos.

Format Compression Best Use
JPEG Lossy Complex photographs where small file size is key.
PNG Lossless Graphics requiring transparency or high contrast edges.
WebP Lossy/Lossless Modern web standards, superior compression.
TIFF Uncompressed High-quality printing and medical imaging.

3. AI vs. Human Painted Art

In the context of binary classification (the core of ArtSense), distinguishing between these two requires looking at "Artifacts."

How AI Paints:

AI models like Stable Diffusion use Latent Diffusion. They start with a field of Gaussian noise and slowly refine it based on text prompts. This often leaves "checkerboard" patterns or inconsistencies in lighting that the human eye might miss but a CNN (Convolutional Neural Network) can detect.

How Humans Paint:

Humans use Physical Media (or digital brushes mimicking them). Human art contains intentional strokes, varying pressure, and a logical understanding of anatomy and physics that current AI models occasionally struggle to simulate perfectly.

4. A Brief History of Imagery

  • Pre-1800s: The Camera Obscura allowed artists to project light onto a wall to trace images.
  • 1826: Joseph Niépce created the first permanent photograph using bitumen.
  • 1975: The first digital camera was built at Kodak, weighing 8 pounds and recording only 0.01 megapixels.
  • 1990s: The JPEG standard was finalized, revolutionizing the internet.
  • 2020s: The rise of Generative AI, where images are "imagined" rather than "captured."
